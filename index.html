Healthy living is good for you.

a thought for you, our valued clients

Eating on purpose, instead of eating to feel full is a really good idea.
Healthy foods are sometimes a bit more expensive, but the nutritional benefit is well worth the extra cost.
A business might do a cost-benefit analysis, to decide if a venture is worth doing.
Likewise, the benefit to your health, and subsequent reduction in medical bills, is well worth it.
Look at the figures. Check health costs vs medical costs.

Onions and garlic are natural antibiotics.
Whether cooked or raw, they help your immune system to win the fight against germs, viruses and such.

Drink filtered or boiled water where the local water quality is below par.
Cut down on the amount of sugar you have in cereal, tea and coffee.
Herbal teas are refreshing and also good for your health.

Avoid Energy Drinks at all costs. The caffeine levels are off the charts. Beware!

Fresh fruit and veges might cost more than bread, but, as the good book says, "Man cannot live on bread alone."

Water consumption is a debatable topic. Basically, drink enough but not too much.
If you feel a headache coming on, drink some water, it might be just the right solution.

Exercise is of paramount importance. Without exercise, your body can develop all sorts of maladies.
During exercise, your heart pumps faster and therefore enriches your blood with more oxygen.
Muscles and joints get a bit of movement, which is good for their continued conditioning.

Bedridden patients often 'waste away', due to insufficient exercise.
Even sitting and doing arm, leg, torso, shoulder, wrist, ankle, neck and head movements can help.

Be gentle with yourself.
Forgive yourself and others regularly. It's not worth it to stay angry and you can harm your health if you become bitter about some or other wrong-doing.
Love with a genuine, caring, self-giving kind of love - and laugh often.
Laughing can release 'feel good' chemicals in your brain and combat 'anger' chemicals.

Be kind. Do to others what you would like others to do to you.
In short - play nice.

Note:

In my humble opinion, fixed navigation bars can be a nuisance on small screens, where the users are trying to read the information on the screen, only to find that half (or a third) of the small screen is covered by a menu.

Better by far to have a relative navigation bar for small screens with a little 'up arrow' (^) to return to the top of the screen, where the navigation bar is readily visible and available.

Having transparent navigation links is an attempt to cover as little as possible of the underlying information. This was my solution when my original project did not pass the tests due to the relative position in the media query for #nav-bar.

I refer to:-

Your #nav-bar should always be at the top of the viewport.

I realise this project is to test my skills, not my ideas of 'best practice', so I modified it to match the requirements specified.
